The sky had that clean, open look to it, the kind that makes you believe you’ve got room to breathe, that maybe for once the world isn’t lining up to take something from you—but I’ve learned better than that, because skies like that don’t mean peace, they just mean whatever’s coming hasn’t decided to show itself yet, and when the shadows finally twisted into shape and the truth came screaming down at us on wings that shouldn’t have worked, I knew we’d stepped into something that had been waiting long before we arrived.

Then came the voice.

Nani Pupu didn’t need to be seen to make herself known, slipping into the moment like she owned it, reminding us in that quiet, confident way that this wasn’t chaos, this wasn’t chance, this was a continuation, a thread we’d been following whether we realized it or not, and that whatever deal we thought we understood was still tightening around us with every step we took.

Lori rose to meet it, because that’s who she is, carrying reason into places that have already decided they don’t want it, trying to talk the storm down like it might remember what it means to listen, but the gorillas didn’t come for words, and they answered her the only way things like that do, with force that doesn’t hesitate and doesn’t negotiate, sending her spinning out of control in a way that makes your gut tighten before your mind catches up.

She dropped.

Not far. Not long. But enough.

And then she steadied herself, pulled the sky back under her like it had just been a momentary disagreement, and I stopped worrying, because Lori doesn’t lose fights like that unless she chooses to, and that’s not something she’s in the habit of doing.

The gorillas split after that, and that’s when it stopped feeling wild and started feeling planned, with two groups bearing down on us while the others peeled away toward the monastery buildings, moving like they had a purpose that didn’t need explaining, like they already knew where they were going and what they were meant to break when they got there, and I didn’t wait for it to make sense because by then I was already moving, taking to the air and cutting one down before it could react, then pushing forward toward Nephyr, who was already chasing something that mattered.

We caught up to one of the groups forcing their way into a building he swore held his queen, and the fight tightened into something immediate and unforgiving, steel and motion and no room for hesitation, and just when it felt like the ground might give out beneath us, the doors burst open and a young giantess came through like she’d been waiting for something worth hitting, striking one of the gorillas with a kind of force that didn’t ask permission, didn’t slow down, just acted, and for a moment, the tide shifted.

Below, the others held the line, even if the climb wasn’t doing them any favors, but Lori solved that problem the way only she could, sending that mop down like it belonged in the middle of a battlefield, lifting Balgus and Bowmore into the fight with a kind of quiet certainty that I’ve stopped questioning because somehow, against all odds, it works, and MODMOS moved ahead of us, silent and precise, clearing a room so completely that by the time we reached him, there was nothing left to fight, just the aftermath and the sense that we were the ones struggling to keep up.

We pushed upward after that, because whatever was driving this hadn’t finished yet, and I took the stairs fast, cutting past one, dropping another, chasing the last of them to the upper level where one finally turned to face me, not with fear or fury, but with that same voice slipping out of it like it had always been there, Nani Pupu speaking through it as if we were just another part of her story.

She said we’d been naughty.

Like this was all just a game she was playing at a distance, like we were pieces she hadn’t finished moving yet, and before I could answer, before I could even decide if there was anything worth saying back, the remaining gorillas pulled away and disappeared into the sky, leaving us with a victory that didn’t feel like one.

We regrouped with Nephyr and met the queen, Thraukha, and the air shifted the way it always does when someone carries real authority, not loud, not obvious, just enough to remind you that you’re no longer the one deciding how things go, and while the conversation around her came fast and layered, what stayed with me was simple, the way she looked at me and spoke like she’d already made her decision.

She needed my help.

Didn’t ask.

Told me.

And maybe that should have bothered me more than it did, but when you’ve spent enough time in fights that don’t have clean endings, you start to understand that sometimes people don’t choose you because they know you, they choose you because they see something you’ve been trying not to acknowledge, and once it’s out there, it’s hard to pretend it isn’t real.

We took what time we could to understand the shape of things, and it wasn’t a comforting picture, with the old giant rulers gone and the birdfolk left to hold together something that was never meant for them, raising the next generation in the shadow of something broken, while the rest of giantkind drifts without direction, waiting for something to give them a reason to become whole again, and in the middle of all that sat the black orchid, a fragile solution to a problem that doesn’t want simple answers.

It could take a small group to Omu, no more than eight, a shortcut through the sky to the place that keeps pulling us forward whether we’re ready or not, but Guruk is still out there, still counting on a promise we made, and time doesn’t care how complicated your choices are, it just keeps moving.

So we chose the road.

Stormreach first.

Slower. But ours.

And somewhere out there, beyond the mountains and the distance we still have to cover, Nani Pupu is watching, waiting, letting us think we’re moving on our own terms when really we’re just stepping exactly where she expects us to.

She’s not done with us.

Not even close.
